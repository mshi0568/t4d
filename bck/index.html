FRONTAL LOBE

The frontal lobe helps us make decisions, make plans and solve problems. It tells us the right or wrong way to behave; for example, that we shouldn’t be rude to other people. It helps us talk, and to initiate or start things.
Examples:
- Nan just says what she thinks now; when she used to be shy before.
- Dad doesn’t say much anymore at all now.
- When I ask Tony what he would like to eat, he says he doesn’t know.
- I ask Tom to put his shoes on, and even though he seems to understand, he just sits there.

Parietal Lobe

The parietal lobe helps us put actions in the right order, such as knowing to put our undies on before our t-shirt and jeans. It also helps us to know where our body is in space and how close we are to things. This helps us to walk around things in our path, and to pick things up.
Examples:
- I know how to get dressed and make a cup of tea, but sometimes I do it in the wrong order or miss a step, like put my cardigan on before my t-shirt, or forget to add water to the cup.
- I’ve feel like I’ve become clumsier and keep tripping or knocking over things.

Temporal Lobe

The temporal lobe is where we make and keep our memories – it is how we remember things. It also helps us to understand what people are saying, and to pay attention, or focus on things.
Examples:
- I have real trouble remembering what I did five minutes ago.
- I’ll be talking to her and I can see that her attention has just drifted off.
- Mum doesn’t remember that dad has passed away, but remembers her wedding in lots of detail.
- I sometimes say something and he doesn’t understand me, like yesterday she didn’t know what “TV” meant.

Occipital Lobe

The occipital lobe is responsible for making sense of the information that comes in from our eyes. For example the visual information that a round circle with two black dots in the middle is a face.
Example:
- Sometimes grandpa doesn’t recognise me, and once he told me to get out of his house.

Cerebellum

The cerebellum looks after things that our bodies do automatically or without thinking – such as breathing and walking. It is responsible for balance and movement.
Example:
- Mum finds it difficult to stand now without our support, otherwise she would fall over.

Brain Stem

You use your frontal lobe nearly everyday. You use it to make decisions, such as what to eat or drink for breakfast in the morning, as well as for thinking or studying for a test. The frontal lobe is also where our personality is formed and where we can carry out higher mental processes such as planning. In addition, the frontal lobe is necessary to being able to speak fluently (without fault) and meaningfully.
